\section{Introduction}

When choosing to dine out and explore a city's restaurant scene, people
consider many factors. Some want cuisine they cannot cook at home. Others
seek a particular atmosphere or price point. But virtually everyone cares
about food safety, even if they rarely think about it explicitly.

New York City makes that concern concrete. Since July 2010, the
Department of Health and Mental Hygiene (DOHMH) has assigned every
restaurant a letter grade based on a numerical inspection score, where a
lower score indicates better performance. A score of 13 or below earns an
``A,'' 14 to 27 earns a ``B,'' and 28 or above earns a ``C.'' These
grades are posted on placards at restaurant entrances, making them
unusually visible. A restaurant that earns anything other than an A pays a
real reputational cost, and that cost is particularly sharp just above
the boundary at 13.

In 2014, data journalist Ben Wellington published an analysis identifying
a suspicious spike in scores at exactly 13, the last score that qualifies
for an A \cite{wellington2014}. Wellington's hypothesis was that inspectors
exercise some degree of informal discretion near grade boundaries.
The NYC Health Department responded that ``inspectors are not instructed
to offer leniency, just to cite what they see.'' Wellington's data,
however, only covered 2010 to 2014. Whether the pattern still exists, and
whether it has grown or shrunk, remained an open question.

This study revisits that question with data updated through 2026 and
extends the analysis in four directions. First, we formally test whether
the grade-boundary anomaly persists and quantify its magnitude using a
more rigorous density discontinuity approach, along with bootstrap
confidence intervals. Second, we examine whether the pattern exists at the
level of individual restaurants, asking whether specific establishments
systematically receive scores near the boundary more often than would be
expected by chance. Third, we assess whether borough and cuisine type
explain meaningful variation in inspection scores using regression and
a two-way blocked ANOVA. Fourth, we examine whether violation types are
distributed uniformly across boroughs and which violations most strongly
predict failing an A grade.

The public health stakes are real. Firestone and Hedberg (2018)
demonstrated that NYC's letter-grading program reduced Salmonella
infection rates in the city by 5.3\% per year relative to the rest of
New York State following its launch. Understanding what drives variation
in grades connects directly to those health outcomes.

\section{Data}

\subsection{Sources}

The primary dataset is the DOHMH New York City Restaurant Inspection
Results file, downloaded from NYC OpenData on April 3, 2026. It contains
113,571 records. The data is structured with one row per violation per
inspection, so a single inspection event appears multiple times when
multiple violations were cited. Key variables include borough, cuisine
type, inspection score, inspection date, violation code, and inspection
type (initial cycle, re-inspection, reopening, and others).

Because violation codes alone are not self-explanatory, we used a
secondary NYC Health Department reference file that maps each code to a
descriptive category such as ``Pest Control,'' ``Cold Holding,'' or
``Handwash / Toilet.'' We also merged neighborhood-level American
Community Survey estimates at the 2020 Neighborhood Tabulation Area (NTA)
level, including median worker earnings and total population, to provide
socioeconomic context for each restaurant's location.

\subsection{Preparation}

Several preprocessing decisions were made to ensure the analysis is
methodologically sound.

\textit{Deduplication.} Because the raw data assigns one row per violation
per inspection event, a single inspection visit with five citations
produces five identical score values. For analyses that operate on scores
(regressions, temporal trends), we deduplicate to one row per
inspection event by grouping on restaurant identifier and inspection date
and retaining the first score. This ensures that inspections with more
violations are not weighted more heavily than cleaner inspections.

\textit{Filtering.} Staten Island was excluded due to sparse cell counts
in the balanced ANOVA design. Inspections with missing scores were
removed. Cuisine types were grouped into ten \textit{hypercategories}
to reduce dimensionality: American, East Asian, South \& Southeast Asian,
Latin American, European, Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, African,
Dish-Type, and Beverages \& Sweets. The Dietary Style category
(Vegan, Vegetarian, etc.) was also excluded from the ANOVA.

\textit{Temporal coverage.} While the dataset nominally spans 2007 through
2026, only 2022 through 2025 contains enough data for inference. After
deduplication to one row per inspection event, fewer than 90 initial
inspections per year exist before 2022, compared to over 1,900 per year
from 2022 onward. This sparse pre-2022 coverage is likely a result of the
NTA merge: the ACS NTA boundaries are defined at the 2020 vintage, and
older inspection records may not have matched cleanly. All temporal trend
analyses, including the A-grade rate figures and the OLS trend regression,
are restricted to 2022--2025 for this reason. The full-period score
distribution (Figure~\ref{fig:score_dist}) uses all available records
since it does not require year-level inference.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Density Discontinuity at Grade Cutoffs}

To test whether score concentrations at 12--13 are anomalous, we use a
density discontinuity approach. Rather than comparing to a flat baseline
(which assumes scores are uniformly distributed, an implausible
assumption), we fit a degree-4 polynomial to the score histogram
\textit{excluding} scores 8 through 16. This captures the smooth overall
shape of the distribution without being influenced by any boundary
effects. The fitted polynomial is then extrapolated into the boundary
region to provide a local expected count at each score.

The excess at scores 12--13 is defined as observed minus expected under
this smooth counterfactual. We compute a 95\% bootstrap confidence
interval for the excess using 2,000 stratified resamples of the full
inspection records.

\subsection{Restaurant-Level Boundary Analysis}

To determine whether the boundary anomaly reflects a population-wide
pattern or whether specific restaurants systematically receive near-cutoff
scores, we compute for each restaurant the fraction of its initial-cycle
inspections that landed at exactly 12 or 13. We restrict to restaurants
with at least two initial inspections. Under the null hypothesis that
score outcomes are random, this fraction should be approximately equal
to the overall marginal rate at which inspections land at 12 or 13.
We test this using a one-sample $t$-test against the observed null rate.

\subsection{OLS Regression and Blocked ANOVA}

To quantify the contributions of borough, cuisine, and time on inspection
score, we fit an OLS regression with borough (Bronx baseline), cuisine
hypercategory (African baseline), and year as predictors on the
deduplicated inspection-level data. Confidence intervals are reported at
95\%.

We complement the regression with a two-way balanced ANOVA:
\[
  \text{Score}_{ijk} = \mu + \alpha_i + \beta_j + (\alpha\beta)_{ij} + \varepsilon_{ijk}
\]
with $n = 30$ observations sampled per hypercategory-borough cell.
The balanced design prevents the extreme statistical power of the full
dataset from producing significant results for trivially small effects.

Inspection scores are right-skewed and likely violate the normality
assumption of ANOVA. We therefore treat the ANOVA as exploratory and
report a Kruskal-Wallis test as a non-parametric robustness check.
ANOVA residual diagnostics are provided in the Appendix.

\subsection{Violation Type by Borough}

A contingency table of violation category by borough was constructed and
tested with Pearson's chi-square test of independence. Violation rates
per borough (as a percentage of all inspections in that borough) are
visualized as a heatmap.

\subsection{Violation-Level Logistic Regression}

We ask an inferential question: given that a violation was cited during
an inspection, does the type of violation predict whether the restaurant
earned an A grade? This is distinct from the predictive question of
forecasting outcomes before an inspection. We fit a logistic regression
with violation category, borough, cuisine, and neighborhood income as
predictors. Coefficients for violation categories provide a controlled
ranking of which violation types are most strongly associated with
failing an A.

\subsection{Predictive Modeling}

To assess how well a restaurant can be predicted to earn or fail an A
grade using only pre-inspection information, we train and compare three
classifiers: logistic regression, random forest (200 trees, max depth 6),
and gradient boosting (200 trees, max depth 4, learning rate 0.05).
Features are: borough, cuisine hypercategory, neighborhood median
earnings, neighborhood population, and year.

All models use an 80/20 stratified train-test split. Logistic regression
predictors are standardized on the training set. Performance is evaluated
using AUC, accuracy, and confusion matrices on the held-out test set.
A majority-class baseline (always predicting the more common class) is
reported as the lower reference bound.

\section{Results}

\subsection{The Grade-Boundary Pattern Is Large and Statistically Robust}

Figure~\ref{fig:score_dist} shows the full inspection score distribution.
The spike at score 13 is visually prominent, as is a smaller peak at
score 28 (the B/C boundary). Both are patterns one would expect if
inspectors exercise some degree of discretion near grade thresholds.
The NYC Health Department's denial notwithstanding, the data are
consistent with this interpretation.

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{fig1_score_distribution}
  \caption{Distribution of NYC restaurant inspection scores (all
    inspection types, 2010--2026). Green bars are A-grade (score $\leq
    13$), blue are B-grade, and red are C-grade. The dashed line shows
    the expected flat baseline, estimated from scores 14--21.}
  \label{fig:score_dist}
\end{figure*}

Figure~\ref{fig:mccrary} shows the more rigorous density discontinuity
analysis. Fitting a degree-4 polynomial to the distribution while
excluding the boundary region (scores 8--16) yields an expected count
of approximately 4,129 inspections at scores 12 and 13 combined. The
observed count is 17,863, an excess of 13,734 inspections (+333\%).
The bootstrap 95\% confidence interval for this excess is
[13,472, 13,996], which does not include zero or any value close to it.
This result is robust to the choice of polynomial degree and the width
of the exclusion window.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{fig_mccrary}

    smooth polynomial counterfactual (dashed line), which was fit
    excluding the boundary region. The gap at scores 12--13 represents

    \textit{Right:} Bootstrap distribution of the excess estimate under

    confirms the result is not sensitive to sampling variability.}
  \label{fig:mccrary}

The spike replicates separately within each borough (Appendix~D,
Figure~\ref{fig:bump_borough}): Bronx +359\%, Brooklyn +497\%, Manhattan
+504\%, Queens +519\%. The excess does not shrink in Manhattan or in
higher-income boroughs, which is inconsistent with the idea that the
pattern is driven by less-monitored areas alone.

\subsection{Individual Restaurants Cluster Near the Boundary}

Among the 5,059 restaurants with at least two initial-cycle inspections,
the mean fraction of inspections landing at scores 12 or 13 is 21.1\%.
The expected fraction under the null hypothesis, derived from the overall
marginal rate of scores 12--13 in the data, is 17.5\%. A one-sample $t$-test comparing the observed restaurant-level rates to
this null yields $t = 9.12$ ($p < 0.001$).

This rules out the possibility that the aggregate spike is driven by a
handful of extreme outliers. The tendency to score near 13 is spread
across many individual restaurants. It also raises the question of
mechanism: is this inspector discretion, restaurant behavior, or some
combination? Restaurants may time their inspections, make targeted
corrections before a scheduled visit, or have learned over time which
violations are most likely to push their score just over the cutoff.
The data cannot distinguish between these explanations, but the
restaurant-level pattern is consistent with strategic behavior on both
sides of the interaction.

We note a limitation: the null rate used here (the aggregate marginal rate
of scoring 12--13 in the full dataset) does not account for heterogeneous
restaurant quality or the dependence of repeated inspections within the
same establishment. The test should be read as showing that restaurants
land at scores 12--13 more often than implied by the aggregate marginal
rate, not as a formal test against a fully specified null model.

Figure~\ref{fig:threshold} shows the distribution of restaurant-level
boundary rates, with the null rate and observed mean indicated.

\subsection{Inspection Scores Have Worsened Since 2022}

Among initial-cycle inspections from 2022 to 2025, mean score increased
from 16.3 in 2022 to 19.0 in 2025 (Figure~\ref{fig:temporal}). An OLS
regression of annual mean score on year estimates the rate of increase at
0.89 points per year ($R^2 = 0.96$, $p = 0.022$). This trend is based on
only four annual data points, so inference should be treated as
suggestive rather than definitive. That said, the trend is consistent
across all four boroughs and statistically significant even at this
resolution.

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{fig3_temporal_trend}
  \caption{Mean and median inspection score by year (2022--2025), initial
    cycle inspections only, deduplicated to one row per inspection event.
    Shaded region is a 95\% CI on the mean. The green dotted line is the
    A-grade cutoff at 13. The dash-dot line is the OLS linear fit
    (slope $= +0.89$ pts/yr, $R^2 = 0.96$).}
  \label{fig:temporal}
\end{figure*}

Despite the rising mean, the median score remains near 12 to 13
throughout this period. This is exactly what one would expect if the
boundary spike is ongoing: the concentration of scores at 12--13 pins the
median near the grade cutoff even as the rest of the distribution shifts
upward.

A-grade rates by borough from 2022 to 2025 are shown in Appendix~D
(Figure~\ref{fig:agrade_boro}). Manhattan maintains the highest rate
throughout (roughly 60--63\%), while Queens has the lowest and shows
the steepest decline (dropping to about 52\% by 2025). All boroughs
trend downward, consistent with the overall score worsening.

\subsection{Borough and Cuisine Effects Are Present but Modest}

The OLS regression of inspection score on borough, cuisine, and year
yields $R^2 = 0.036$. Borough, cuisine, and time together explain less
than 4\% of score variance. The large majority of variation reflects
factors specific to individual restaurants on individual inspection days.

Within this limited explanatory power, some patterns hold up. After
controlling for cuisine composition and year, Queens scores are
significantly higher (worse) than the Bronx baseline ($\hat{\beta} =
1.64$, $p < 0.001$), as are Brooklyn restaurants ($\hat{\beta} = 0.86$,
$p = 0.038$). Manhattan is not significantly different from the Bronx
once cuisine and year are accounted for ($\hat{\beta} = 0.12$, $p = 0.77$).
Manhattan's raw advantage in score distributions therefore appears to
reflect its cuisine mix rather than better sanitation per se.

Cuisine hypercategory is a stronger predictor than borough. All
hypercategories score better than African cuisine restaurants, which serve
as the baseline, by margins of roughly 3 to 9 points (all $p < 0.05$
except East Asian and South \& SE Asian). The full coefficient plot is
in Appendix~D (Figure~\ref{fig:ols_coefs}).

The two-way balanced ANOVA ($n = 30$ per cell) confirms these patterns.
Cuisine hypercategory shows a significant effect ($F(9,1160) = 4.12$,
$p < 0.001$) and the cuisine-by-borough interaction is also significant
($F(27,1160) = 1.54$, $p = 0.038$). Borough alone is only marginally
significant ($F(3,1160) = 2.25$, $p = 0.081$). As a non-parametric
robustness check, the Kruskal-Wallis test strongly rejects the null
hypothesis that score distributions are identical across boroughs
($H = 384.86$, $p < 0.001$), suggesting that the ANOVA borough effect,
while marginal by $F$-test, reflects a real distributional difference.
Full ANOVA output and residual diagnostics are in Appendices B and C.

\subsection{Violation Types Vary Substantially Across Boroughs}

The chi-square test of independence for violation category by borough
strongly rejects the null ($\chi^2 = 678.2$, $df = 66$,
$p \approx 2 \times 10^{-102}$). Different parts of the city face
different food safety challenges. Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap} shows the
percentage of inspections in each borough that received each violation
category.

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{fig5_violation_by_borough}
  \caption{Violation category rate by borough, as a percentage of all
    inspections in that borough. Darker shading indicates higher rates.
    Pest Control violations are most concentrated in the Bronx, while
    Cooling \& Refrigeration and Hot Holding violations show elevated
    rates in Manhattan and Queens, respectively.}

Pest Control violations are most elevated in the Bronx. Cooling and
Refrigeration violations are higher in Manhattan, possibly reflecting
the prevalence of restaurants with extensive refrigeration equipment in
dense commercial corridors. Food Workers violations are elevated in
Brooklyn. These patterns likely reflect a combination of building
infrastructure, restaurant type composition, and neighborhood
environment, rather than differences in inspection rigor alone.

\subsection{Which Violations Best Predict Failing an A?}

Figure~\ref{fig:viol_coefs} shows violation-level logistic regression
coefficients, controlling for borough, cuisine, and neighborhood income.
Pest Control violations have the largest negative coefficient, meaning
that a restaurant receiving a Pest Control citation is most strongly
associated with not earning an A grade. Permit, Handwash / Toilet, Cold
Holding, and Hot Holding violations follow. On the positive end,
Maintenance and Plumbing violations are associated with a higher
probability of earning an A. Structural and plumbing deficiencies tend to be lower-severity items
cited at otherwise compliant restaurants, while temperature and pest
violations carry heavier point penalties and are harder to fix in place.

\subsection{Prior Grade Predicts Future Score Trajectory}

One question the data can answer directly is whether receiving a particular
grade in one inspection tells us anything about what happens next. We matched
each restaurant's consecutive initial-cycle inspection pairs and computed score
changes by prior grade.

The results are in Figure~\ref{fig:trajectory}. Restaurants that earned a C
improved dramatically by their next visit, falling by 21.2 points on average
(vs.\ A-grade change: $t = -51.95$, $p \approx 0$). Restaurants that had
earned a B also improved, but more modestly: mean next-inspection score dropped
by 2.4 points. The median next score for B-earners landed at exactly 13 ---
just inside the A-grade boundary. Restaurants that had already earned an A
drifted slightly upward (worse), by an average of 6.0 points.

The probability of earning an A on the next inspection tracks the same
gradient: 70\% for restaurants coming off an A, 56\% for B earners, and 44\%
for C earners ($t$ for B vs.\ A: $-19.53$, $p \approx 0$).

The B-grade trajectory is the most striking. When a restaurant receives a B,
its next score most often lands right at 13. This mirrors the aggregate spike
in the cross-sectional distribution and suggests the boundary effect is not
just a one-off statistical artifact but plays out repeatedly within individual
restaurants over time. Whether it reflects targeted compliance corrections,
inspector discretion, or some combination, the data cannot say. But the
pattern is hard to dismiss.

\subsection{Pre-Inspection Context Cannot Meaningfully Predict Grade}

Table~\ref{tab:models} compares all predictive models on the held-out
test set. All three classifiers (logistic regression, random forest,
and gradient boosting) achieve AUC values between 0.597 and 0.602,
clustered tightly just above the majority-class baseline of AUC $= 0.500$.
Random forest and gradient boosting, despite their greater model
complexity, provide essentially no improvement over the simpler logistic
regression.

That all three models land at AUC $\approx 0.60$ rules out the simplest
alternative explanation: that the logistic regression was underperforming
due to model misspecification. It was not. The pre-inspection features
just do not contain enough information to predict inspection outcomes,
regardless of how the classifier is specified. The bottom row of the table shows that
adding violation type (information available only during the inspection)
does provide a meaningful lift to AUC $= 0.673$, confirming that the
limitation is in the feature set rather than the modeling approach.

\section{Model Validation and Diagnostics}

All logistic regression models were evaluated on stratified 80/20
hold-out splits, with standardization fit only on training data. No
information from the test set entered model training or preprocessing.
The reported AUC values are therefore honest out-of-sample estimates.

For the ANOVA, we used balanced sampling ($n = 30$ per cell) to avoid
conflating statistical significance with practical importance. As an
additional check, the Kruskal-Wallis test ($H = 384.86$, $p < 0.001$)
confirms that borough differences in score distributions are real and
not an artifact of parametric assumptions. Appendix C shows the ANOVA
residual distribution and a normal Q-Q plot; the residuals are clearly
non-normal (right-skewed), which reinforces our decision to treat the
ANOVA as exploratory and to rely on the Kruskal-Wallis test for the
inferential claim.

The OLS regression for the temporal trend is based on four annual
mean scores (2022--2025). While the $R^2 = 0.96$ and $p = 0.022$
are formally significant, inference from a four-point regression
should be treated as suggestive. We report it as a descriptive summary
of the trend rather than a formal test.

The density discontinuity analysis was subjected to explicit uncertainty
quantification through bootstrapping. The 95\% CI for the excess at
scores 12--13 is [13,472, 13,996], with essentially no probability
mass near zero. We acknowledge that the choice of polynomial degree and
exclusion window are somewhat arbitrary. We tested polynomial degrees 2
through 5 and exclusion windows of $\pm 6$ and $\pm 8$ scores around
the cutoff; the excess estimate ranged from approximately 12,800 to 14,200
across all combinations, and the 95\% bootstrap CI never approached zero.
The substantive conclusion is insensitive to these choices, even if the
exact number is not.

\section{Conclusion}

Several findings emerge from this analysis.

The grade-boundary anomaly first identified by Wellington in 2014 remains
large, statistically unambiguous, and consistent across all four boroughs.
Using a polynomial smooth counterfactual rather than a flat baseline, we
estimate a surplus of roughly 13,700 inspections at scores 12--13
above what the surrounding distribution would predict (+333\%, 95\% CI:
[13,472, 13,996]). The same pattern shows up at the level of individual
restaurants: establishments land at scores 12--13 more often than
implied by the aggregate marginal rate ($t = 9.12$, $p < 0.001$). The
boundary effect also appears in consecutive inspection trajectories.
When a restaurant earns a B, its next inspection score most frequently
lands at exactly 13. This suggests the boundary spike is not just a
cross-sectional artifact but repeats within individual restaurants
over time. The data are consistent with discretionary scoring near grade
thresholds, though observational data alone cannot confirm the mechanism.

Inspection scores have worsened over the period with reliable data (2022
to 2025), increasing at roughly 0.9 points per year across all boroughs.
The A-grade rate has declined modestly in every borough. Whether this
reflects genuine deterioration in restaurant hygiene, changes in
inspection stringency, or shifts in the restaurant population remains an
open question.

Different parts of the city face different kinds of food safety challenges.
Violation type distributions are strongly non-uniform across boroughs
($\chi^2 = 678.2$, $p \approx 2 \times 10^{-102}$), with pest and
temperature-control violations showing the greatest geographic variation.
Among violation types, Pest Control and temperature-holding violations
most strongly predict failing an A grade, while structural and plumbing
violations are associated with passing.

Perhaps the most practically important finding is that pre-inspection
context has almost no predictive power. Three different classifiers,
including a random forest and a gradient boosting model, all converge
near AUC $= 0.60$ using borough, cuisine, and neighborhood income as
features. This is only marginally better than random guessing. What a
restaurant does and how it is managed on the day of inspection determines
outcomes far more than where it is located or what kind of food it serves.

Future work could examine whether the grade-boundary pattern varies by
inspector team or inspection season, incorporate restaurant fixed effects
to separate individual trajectories from city-wide shifts, or link
inspection records to consumer review data to assess whether declining
A-grade rates translate to behavioral changes by diners.

\section{Literature Review}

Our project was motivated by two bodies of work. The first is Wellington's
(2014) original analysis identifying the boundary spike at score 13.
The second is the public health literature on whether these grades produce
real health improvements.

Firestone and Hedberg (2018) examined Salmonella infection rates in NYC
versus the rest of New York State from 1994 to 2015. Using a
quasi-experimental interrupted time-series design with negative binomial
regression, they found that NYC's Salmonella rates declined by 5.3\% per
year relative to the rest of the state following the grading program's
launch in July 2010 (IRR $= 0.95$, 95\% CI: $0.92$--$0.98$, $p < 0.01$).
Before 2010, NYC's rates were significantly higher than the statewide
average. By 2011--2015, they were statistically indistinguishable.

The authors note several limitations. The grading program coincided with
other policy changes (more frequent inspections, higher fines, expanded
public resources), making it impossible to attribute the Salmonella
decline to letter grades alone. The analysis is also ecological:
case counts are based on county of residence, not where individuals ate.
Despite these caveats, the study provides strong motivation for our
project. If letter grades are associated with population-level reductions
in foodborne illness, then understanding what drives grade variation
becomes a question with direct public health implications.
