\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

The New York City Department of Health and Mental Hygiene (DOHMH)
grades every restaurant in the five boroughs through a point-based
health inspection system. A score of 0--13 earns an ``A,'' 14--27 a
``B,'' and 28 or above a ``C.'' In 2014, data journalist Ben Wellington
published an analysis showing a suspicious spike in inspection scores
at exactly 13---the last score that earns an A grade---suggesting that
inspectors may exercise subjective leniency near the grade boundary
\cite{wellington2014}.

Our study revisits this claim using updated data spanning 2010--2026 and
extends it in three directions. First, we formally test whether the
grade-boundary spike persists and quantify its magnitude. Second, we ask
whether a restaurant's borough and cuisine type explain meaningful
variation in inspection scores, using a two-way blocked ANOVA design.
Third, we investigate whether violation types are distributed uniformly
across boroughs, or whether geography predicts which types of health
hazards restaurants are cited for. Finally, as a predictive exercise, we
assess how well pre-inspection characteristics alone can forecast
whether a restaurant will earn an A.

\section{Data Collection and Data Description}
% ============================================================

We used two datasets from NYC's OpenData program. The primary dataset is
the DOHMH New York City Restaurant Inspection Results
(updated April 3, 2026), containing 113,571 records---one row per
violation per inspection---covering 19,065 unique restaurants.
Key variables include borough, cuisine type, inspection score, inspection
date, violation code, and inspection type (initial cycle, re-inspection,
etc.).

Because the violation code field is not self-explanatory, we used a
secondary NYC Health Department reference file mapping each violation
code to a descriptive category (e.g., ``PEST CONTROL,''
``COLD HOLDING,'' ``HANDWASH/TOILET'').

We also merged neighborhood-level American Community Survey estimates:
median earnings (\texttt{MdEWrkE}) and total population (\texttt{Pop\_1E})
at the 2020 Neighborhood Tabulation Area (NTA) level.

\subsection*{Data Preparation}

The raw data was filtered to remove Staten Island (sparse) and
inspections with missing scores. Cuisine types were grouped into ten
\emph{hypercategories} (American, East Asian, South \& Southeast Asian,
Latin American, European, Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, African,
Dish-Type, and Beverages \& Sweets) to reduce dimensionality and improve
interpretability. After cleaning, the analysis dataset contains
101,840 inspection records.

\section{Statistical Models}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Formal Test of Grade-Boundary Spike}

To assess whether the spike at score 13 is statistically meaningful, we
compared observed inspection counts in the 12--21 score range to an
expected uniform (flat) distribution, estimated using the mean count at
scores 14--21 as a baseline. A chi-square goodness-of-fit test was used
to evaluate whether observed counts deviate significantly from this
expected flat distribution.

\subsection{Temporal Trend}

We restricted to initial-cycle inspections between 2015 and 2025 and
computed the mean and median score by year. Because every inspection
in the same event shares the same score, this aggregation avoids
double-counting. A simple OLS regression of mean score on year was used
to estimate the trend slope.

\subsection{Two-Way Blocked ANOVA (Inferential)}

To assess whether borough and cuisine type explain meaningful variance in
inspection scores, we fit a two-way balanced ANOVA:
\[
  \text{Score}_{ijk} = \mu + \alpha_i + \beta_j + (\alpha\beta)_{ij} + \varepsilon_{ijk}
\]
where $\alpha_i$ denotes the borough effect, $\beta_j$ the
hypercategory effect, and $(\alpha\beta)_{ij}$ their interaction.
To satisfy the balance requirement and avoid the influence of very
large sample sizes on $p$-values, we sampled $n$ observations per
cell, excluding sparsely populated combinations (Staten Island;
Dietary Style hypercategory).

\subsection{Violation Type by Borough ($\chi^2$ Test)}

To answer Research Question 3---whether certain violation categories are
disproportionately associated with particular boroughs---we constructed
a contingency table of violation category $\times$ borough and applied
Pearson's chi-square test of independence.

\subsection{Predictive Model: Logistic Regression (Pre-Inspection Features)}

To honestly assess predictive power, we built a logistic regression model
using only features knowable \emph{before} an inspection occurs: borough,
cuisine hypercategory, neighborhood median earnings, neighborhood
population, and year of inspection. The outcome is grade binary
($1 = $ A grade, score $\leq 13$). We used an 80/20 stratified train/test
split, with standardized predictors. Model performance is evaluated via
AUC and accuracy.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Grade-Boundary Spike Persists and Has Grown}

\begin{figure}[ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{fig2_bump_test}
  \caption{Zoomed view of inspection score distribution (scores 5--25).
    The dashed line shows the expected count per score under a flat
    distribution estimated from scores 14--21. The A/B boundary spike
    is dramatic and statistically conclusive.}
  \label{fig:bump}
\end{figure}

The chi-square test is conclusive: $\chi^2 = 30{,}791.6$, $p \approx 0$
(scores 12--21 compared to expected flat baseline). Observed inspections
at scores 12--13 total 17,863 versus an expected 3,033---a
\textbf{489\% excess}. Wellington's 2014 analysis found a similar but
smaller pattern; our updated data shows the phenomenon has not abated.

\subsection{Scores Are Worsening Over Time}

\begin{figure}[ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{fig3_temporal_trend}
  \caption{Mean and median inspection score by year, restricted to
    initial-cycle inspections. Shaded band is a 95\% confidence interval
    on the mean. Lower scores are better; the A-grade cutoff (13) is
    shown in green.}
  \label{fig:temporal}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:temporal} reveals a striking and previously unreported
trend: initial-cycle inspection scores have risen substantially from a
mean of approximately 15 in 2015 to nearly 28 in 2025. The median score
also increased from 16 to 23 over the same period. % TODO: add OLS slope and p-value
This suggests that either restaurant hygiene has genuinely deteriorated,

explained by these factors is small relative to residual within-cell
variation, suggesting that individual restaurant practices dominate
over geography and cuisine type.

The chi-square test strongly rejects independence between violation type
and borough ($\chi^2 = 678.2$, $df = 66$, $p < 0.001$). As shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap}, the Bronx shows higher rates of \texttt{PEST
CONTROL} violations, while Manhattan has elevated rates of
\texttt{COOLING \& REFRIGERATION} violations. Queens and Brooklyn show
distinctive patterns in food-holding violations.

The pre-inspection logistic regression achieves an AUC of 0.60 and
accuracy of 59.4\% on the held-out test set (Table~\ref{tab:model_compare}).
This near-chance performance implies that restaurant location, cuisine
type, and neighborhood income explain very little of whether a restaurant
will earn an A grade. In other words, \emph{what a restaurant does}---not
where it is or what it serves---determines its inspection outcome.

\section{Model Validation and Generalization}
% ============================================================

The logistic regression was evaluated on a stratified 80/20 hold-out
split to preserve the class distribution. Predictors were standardized
using training-set parameters only, with no information leakage to the
test set. The modest AUC (0.60) is robust: it reflects the true
difficulty of predicting inspection outcomes from pre-inspection
characteristics alone, not an artifact of our modeling choices.

The ANOVA results should be interpreted cautiously. The sample is
extremely large, so even trivially small effects produce significant
$p$-values. We emphasized balanced sampling across cells (following a
block design) to make the test more interpretable. The practical
significance of borough and cuisine effects remains limited.

% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
% ============================================================

Our analysis yields four main findings:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Grading leniency at the A/B boundary is large and ongoing.}
    Nearly 15,000 inspections appear at scores 12--13 in excess of what a
    fair distribution would predict---a 489\% surplus. Wellington's 2014
    observation was not a historical artifact.

  \item \textbf{Inspection scores have worsened substantially since 2015.}
    Mean initial-cycle scores rose from $\approx 15$ to $\approx 28$
    over ten years, a trend that warrants further investigation into
    whether it reflects genuine deterioration in restaurant hygiene,
    shifts in inspection stringency, or changing restaurant demographics.

  \item \textbf{Violation types are geographically non-uniform.}
    The Bronx, Brooklyn, Manhattan, and Queens show distinct violation
    profiles, with pest control and temperature-related violations
    particularly informative ($\chi^2 = 678.2$, $p < 0.001$).

  \item \textbf{Restaurant location and cuisine alone cannot predict grade.}
    With an AUC of only 0.60, pre-inspection features explain very little
    about inspection outcomes, suggesting that inspection results are
    driven primarily by individual restaurant practices rather than
    neighborhood context.
\end{enumerate}

Limitations include the unit-of-analysis challenge (one row per
violation complicates inspection-level inference) and the possibility of
confounding from inspector identity or inspection timing. Future work
could model inspector fixed effects or examine whether the grade-boundary
spike varies by borough or by inspector team.
